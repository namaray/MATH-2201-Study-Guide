Vectors in 3-Space (Chapters 11.1 - 11.4)

This section covers the fundamentals of the 3D coordinate system, the definition and algebra of vectors, and the two primary ways to multiply vectors: the dot product and the cross product.

Chapter 11.1: 3D Coordinates, Spheres, & Cylindrical Surfaces

Key Concepts

  • 3D Rectangular Coordinates: Points in space are located using an ordered triple (x, y, z). The axes (x, y, z) are mutually perpendicular and oriented by the right-hand rule.
  • Coordinate Planes: Three planes (xy-plane where z=0, xz-plane where y=0, and yz-plane where x=0) divide space into eight octants.
  • Cylindrical Surfaces: An equation with only two variables (e.g., x² + y² = 1) represents a surface in 3D. It is formed by taking the 2D curve and "extruding" it parallel to the axis of the missing variable.

Core Formulas

Distance Formula in 3-Space: The distance d between P₁(x₁, y₁, z₁) and P₂(x₂, y₂, z₂) is:
d = √[(x₂ - x₁)² + (y₂ - y₁)² + (z₂ - z₁)²]
Standard Equation of a Sphere: A sphere with center (h, k, l) and radius r has the equation:
(x - h)² + (y - k)² + (z - l)² = r²

To find the center and radius from a general form, you must complete the square.

Chapter 11.2: Vectors

Key Concepts

  • A vector is a quantity with both magnitude (length) and direction. A scalar is a quantity with only magnitude (a real number).
  • Vectors can be represented in component form v = <v₁, v₂, v₃> or using standard basis vectors v = v₁i + v₂j + v₃k, where i=<1,0,0>, j=<0,1,0>, and k=<0,0,1>.
  • Vector arithmetic (addition, subtraction, scalar multiplication) is performed component-wise.

Core Formulas

Vector between two points: The vector from point P₁(x₁, y₁, z₁) to P₂(x₂, y₂, z₂) is:
P₁P₂ = <x₂ - x₁, y₂ - y₁, z₂ - z₁> (Terminal point minus Initial point)
Norm (Magnitude or Length) of a Vector: The norm of v = <v₁, v₂, v₃> is:
||v|| = √[v₁² + v₂² + v₃²]
Normalizing a Vector (Finding a Unit Vector): To find a unit vector u with the same direction as a non-zero vector v:
u = v / ||v||

Chapter 11.3: The Dot Product

Key Concepts

  • The dot product takes two vectors and produces a scalar.
  • It is primarily used to find the angle between two vectors or to determine if they are orthogonal.
  • Orthogonal Vectors: Two vectors u and v are orthogonal (perpendicular) if and only if their dot product is zero: u · v = 0.
  • Orthogonal Projection: The projection of vector v onto vector b (projbv) is the "shadow" or vector component of v in the direction of b.

Core Formulas

Dot Product (Component Form): For u=<u₁,u₂,u₃> and v=<v₁,v₂,v₃>:
u · v = u₁v₁ + u₂v₂ + u₃v₃
Angle Between Two Vectors: The angle θ between two non-zero vectors u and v is:
cos(θ) = (u · v) / (||u|| ||v||)
Orthogonal Projection of v onto b:
projbv = ( (v · b) / ||b||² ) * b
Key Application: Work. The work W done by a constant force F moving an object along a displacement vector d is W = F · d.

Chapter 11.4: The Cross Product

Key Concepts

  • The cross product takes two vectors in 3-space and produces a new vector. (This is a 3D-only operation!)
  • The resulting vector u x v is orthogonal to both u and v.
  • The direction of u x v is determined by the right-hand rule.
  • The cross product is anti-commutative: u x v = -(v x u).

Core Formulas

Cross Product (Determinant Form): For u=<u₁,u₂,u₃> and v=<v₁,v₂,v₃>:
      | i   j   k  |
u x v = | u₁  u₂  u₃ | = i(u₂v₃ - u₃v₂) - j(u₁v₃ - u₃v₁) + k(u₁v₂ - u₂v₁)
      | v₁  v₂  v₃ |
Magnitude of the Cross Product:
||u x v|| = ||u|| ||v|| sin(θ)
Scalar Triple Product (Volume): The volume V of a parallelepiped with adjacent edges u, v, w is:
V = |u · (v x w)|
Key Application: Area. The area of a parallelogram with adjacent sides u and v is Area = ||u x v||. The area of the triangle with these sides is half of that.
